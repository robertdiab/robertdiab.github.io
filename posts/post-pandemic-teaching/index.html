How teaching will change after the pandemic

2021-04-02

What will teaching be like in the fall of 2021? Will we make a full return to campus? If not, how long it might it take? And what will it look like in the meantime?

A year of serendipitous discoveries

In the past year, having to do so many things differently, I made a host of new discoveries as a teacher. I know I’m not alone.

   

I learned how to make videos about discrete concepts and ideas I teach. I got better at recording lectures and using live sessions to go over fact-patterns and problem questions with students.

I got comfortable with the idea of recording the classes I held live. On at least a few occasions, I looked back at a segment of the class I didn’t think went well, and it was always better than I thought (or not as bad!).

Students liked having the recordings, and they didn’t appear to affect attendance.

What will we keep?

A few colleagues have told me they intend to hold onto to at least some facets of the flipped classroom. One suggested he never wants to hold live lectures again — recorded video seems best for this. I’m on the fence about this. I see the benefits to both live and recorded lectures.

One approach might be to keep the recorded lectures for my upper-year Evidence course, but do brief 10 to 15 minute summaries of the main ideas in person, before launching into a fact-pattern problem we would do together.

It’s hard to imagine students in upper year courses wanting to give up the flexibility and other benefits of having recorded lectures.

It also seems likely that whatever we choose to do in person – whatever balance we strike between synchronous and asynchronous methods – we’re going to have to record everything, given the uncertainty around who can attend.

Some form of hybrid teaching is inevitable. And the longer we use it, the more likely it is to become permanent.
